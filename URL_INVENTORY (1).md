# Wren Law Firm — Content Source of Truth

**Purpose.** This document is the factual foundation for the redesigned Wren Law Firm website. It captures what the firm says about itself, substantiates that with credentials and reputation signals, and gives copywriters, designers, and developers a single, clean reference so the new site is built on accurate, prospect-ready content.

The Wren Law Firm website is the **primary source of truth** for who the firm is, what it does, and how it represents itself. Public directories are used here only to supplement — to add credentials, corroborate contact details, and surface positive reputation signals that belong on the site but currently aren't there.

## 2. The firm

### 2.1 Origin

Wren Law Firm was established on January 1, 1997, by Daniel E. Wren. Before founding the firm, Dan led the litigation department at one of Arkansas's largest personal injury firms. He started Wren Law Firm with a clear focus: consistent, direct communication with clients, alongside strong case outcomes. The firm opened with Dan and one secretary and has grown since.

### 2.2 Mission and approach

Wren Law Firm represents injured individuals across Arkansas: people hurt in car accidents, people hurt on the job, and people who can no longer work due to disabilities. The firm's approach centers on three commitments:

- **Direct client communication.** The firm was founded specifically to address a gap Dan saw at prior firms — clients who felt out of the loop on their own cases.
- **Contingency representation.** *"We only get paid when we obtain a settlement for you."*
- **24/7 availability.** Phone lines are answered around the clock because injuries don't keep business hours.

### 2.3 Voice and positioning

From the site: *"We handle your claim so that you can focus on getting better."* — Dan Wren

The firm positions itself as experienced, accessible, and client-communication-focused. The aesthetic (classical columns, "since 1997" prominence, judicial iconography) signals tradition and credibility rather than high-volume marketing.

## 4. Practice areas

The firm focuses on three core practice areas. All three have dedicated practice pages, primary nav entries, and blog content.

### 4.1 Personal Injury

Representation for people injured in:
- Auto accidents (car, truck, 18-wheeler)
- Motorcycle accidents
- Other bodily injury claims

**Fee structure (from the site):** *"Our standard fee is twenty-five percent of the total amount recovered from the person who caused the accident… significantly lower than many of the other firms in the state which charge upwards of forty percent."* This is a concrete, competitive, prospect-visible commitment and should be kept prominent in the redesign.

**The firm's PI approach (per site):**
1. Home-visit intake — the firm comes to the client, not the other way around
2. Liability assessment and evidence preservation
3. Direct handling of the opposing insurance company while the client focuses on recovery
4. Medical-bill coordination during treatment
5. Settlement pursuit after medical release

### 4.2 Social Security Disability

Representation for:
- Social Security Disability Income (SSDI) applications and appeals
- Supplemental Security Income (SSI) applications and appeals
- Administrative Law Judge hearings

### 4.3 Workers' Compensation

Representation for:
- Job-related injuries
- Lost-wages claims

## 11. Spanish-language templates (Plantillas en español)

This is a first-class design requirement, not an afterthought — Blanca Baker is already on staff as a Spanish Interpreter, and a significant portion of Little Rock's population is Spanish-speaking. The Spanish experience should feel native, not translated-looking.

### 11.2 Language switcher

A language switcher (labeled **"English / Español"**) lives in the site header at every breakpoint, and in the footer as a backup. Tapping the switcher takes the user to the equivalent page in the other language — not always back to the homepage.

The current `Español?` footer link becomes this switcher in the new design.

### 11.3 Design system adjustments for Spanish

The Spanish templates use the **same design system** as English — same colors, same typography, same components. A few accommodations are needed:

- **Type sizing latitude.** Spanish translations typically run 15–30% longer than English. Headlines and button labels that are tight in English should have flexible sizing in Spanish. For example, the homepage hero H1 at 36px may need to step down to 32px or 30px in Spanish to hold the same line count.
- **Button and nav padding.** Primary-nav items ("Áreas de Práctica", "Nuestro Equipo") are longer than their English counterparts. The desktop nav should either accommodate the longer strings or break earlier to hamburger.
- **Form field labels.** Gravity Forms (or its replacement) needs Spanish field labels, Spanish placeholder text, and Spanish validation messages.
- **Date and phone formatting.** Phone numbers stay in US format `(501) 523-1000`. Dates on the Spanish blog should use the `DD de mes de YYYY` convention (e.g., *"2 de abril de 2026"* rather than *"April 2, 2026"*).

### 11.4 Technical requirements

- **HTML `lang` attribute.** English pages: `<html lang="en">`. Spanish pages: `<html lang="es">`.
- **`hreflang` tags on every page.** Each page includes `<link rel="alternate" hreflang="en" href="...">` and `<link rel="alternate" hreflang="es" href="...">` pointing to its counterpart, plus `hreflang="x-default"` pointing to the English version.
- **Sitemap entries.** Both language versions appear in the XML sitemap.
- **Structured data.** The JSON-LD `Organization` and `LegalService` blocks include `inLanguage` specifying both `en` and `es` where appropriate.
- **Consistent slug strategy.** Spanish URLs use Spanish slugs (not translated English slugs in English-language URL paths). Redirects from the old `/area-of-practice/*` URLs to new Spanish URLs only fire when the user's browser language or selection is Spanish.

### 11.5 Translation quality bar

- Translations should be done by a **native Spanish speaker familiar with Arkansas and US legal terminology** — not machine translation alone. Machine translation as a starting draft is fine; human editing by a qualified translator is required before publication.
- **Legal terms-of-art** (SSDI, SSI, Workers' Compensation, Personal Injury) should use the Spanish-language conventions that Spanish-speaking clients actually use when describing these things — not overly formal academic Spanish. Blanca Baker is the natural in-house reviewer for this.
- **Neutral Spanish register.** The firm serves Arkansas, where the Spanish-speaking population is predominantly Mexican-origin. Content should use neutral Latin American Spanish rather than Castilian conventions (*ustedes* over *vosotros*, straightforward vocabulary).

### 11.6 Content parity

Every piece of content on the English site must have a Spanish equivalent, and vice versa. Specifically:

- All attorney bios (Dan Wren, Keith Wren) translated in full.
- All staff cards translated.
- The "Why Wren Law Firm" block translated with fee-transparency language intact (25% vs 40%+ is a conversion driver for Spanish-speaking prospects too).
- All practice-area long-form content translated.
- Blog posts translated as they're published, not retroactively. Spanish-first original content by the firm is also welcome.
- Forms (contact, consultation request, call-back) fully translated including confirmation messages.
- Privacy Policy and Terms of Use professionally translated — not machine output, given their legal status.
